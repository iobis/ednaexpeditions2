about_site: |
  The Malindi–Watamu–Arabuko Sokoke Biosphere Reserve lies on Kenya's north coast around Malindi and Watamu, linking two marine protected areas with the ancient Arabuko Sokoke coastal forest. It is an exceptional mosaic of coral reefs, reef-protected lagoons, seagrass beds, rocky shore habitats and mangrove creeks, alongside one of East Africa's most important remaining tracts of coastal forest.

  These ecosystems are ecologically significant because they support high-value reef fish communities (including threatened species), provide nursery habitat in seagrass and inshore areas, and include a mangrove system noted for exceptional species diversity on the East African coast; the seascape also lies on major cetacean migration routes. Culturally and economically, the reserve underpins livelihoods through fisheries, nature-based tourism and community conservation initiatives.

  Management and conservation efforts are led through Kenya's marine protected area framework (Watamu/Malindi MPAs) and forest stewardship, alongside active monitoring and community-based conservation partners working on reef health and habitat protection.

what_we_plan: |
  Through eDNA Expeditions, our site in the Malindi–Watamu–Arabuko Sokoke Biosphere Reserve will establish a repeatable, community-supported "sentinel monitoring" system that complements existing protected-area management and long-term ecological monitoring. The seascape contains coral reefs and fish assemblages of high conservation value, extensive seagrass nursery areas, mangrove forests with exceptionally high species diversity for East Africa, and important migratory routes for cetaceans—making it both ecologically significant and sensitive to climate and local pressures.

  Our core management questions are practical: how are biodiversity communities on reefs, seagrass beds, and mangroves changing across seasons and through disturbance events (marine heatwaves, coral bleaching, freshwater pulses, sedimentation and pollution), and are the marine protected areas maintaining resilient assemblages over time? We want to track whether key habitats—especially seagrass and mangrove-linked lagoon systems such as Mida Creek—continue to function as nurseries and spawning grounds, and whether community composition signals early warning of declining habitat quality before changes are obvious to visual surveys.

  We will also use eDNA for targeted detection of hard-to-observe or low-density taxa, and for early indication of invasive or emerging problem species, drawing on the growing evidence base that eDNA supports sensitive, non-invasive biodiversity monitoring for management.

  Operationally, sampling will be integrated with our Pwani University–WRTI–KWS monitoring consortium: WRTI's Coastal and Marine Research Centre (Malindi) already conducts long-term ecological monitoring across MPAs including Watamu and Malindi, while KWS management plans provide an established framework for priorities and actions. PUBReC will anchor the genetics/bioinformatics capacity and use the eDNA Expeditions protocols and support systems, while citizen scientists (BMUs, youth groups, community conservation teams) help collect standardised samples multiple times per year, building local stewardship and continuity.

  The local objective is to produce decision-ready, site-specific biodiversity evidence that managers can act on—while ensuring outputs are FAIR and openly shared through the eDNA Expeditions pipeline and published via OBIS, with Kenya's OBIS node hosted by KMFRI, so our results are comparable across regions and useful beyond the site.
