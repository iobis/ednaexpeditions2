about_site: |
  The Manyangue na Elombo-Campo Marine Park is a marine and coastal protected area established in 2021, located on the southeast coast of Cameroon. It stretches 44 km and occupies 110,300 ha of territorial waters contiguous to the maritime border of Equatorial Guinea. The park's diverse and interconnected ecosystems include coastal mangroves, estuaries, sea turtle nesting beaches, seagrass beds, rocky reefs, and kelp forests. Together, they support rich biodiversity and provide essential ecosystem services such as coastal protection for surrounding indigenous communities.

  The park is an area of high ecological importance and serves as an important habitat for a diverse range of marine megafauna. Through citizen science data, several cetacean species, including humpback whales, sperm whales, and the critically endangered Atlantic humpback dolphin, have been observed seasonally. The highly cryptic African manatee is also known to frequent coastal estuaries. Approximately forty species of sharks and rays have been documented in fisheries landing surveys, with at least 11 classified as Endangered or Critically Endangered by the IUCN Red List.

  Scientific diving and marine megafauna monitoring have been initiated to improve baseline understanding of the ecosystem. In parallel, collaborations with TUBE AWU support marine megafauna conservation, sustainable livelihoods, policy development, and outreach initiatives.

what_we_plan: |
  The Manyangue na Elombo-Campo Marine Park remains under-studied, with biodiversity baselines that are still largely unknown or undocumented. Current ecological data are in their infancy and remain insufficient to fully support effective long-term management. In this context, the site would greatly benefit from integrating eDNA methods to generate robust baseline data on species composition and strengthen biodiversity monitoring, thereby directly supporting evidence-based management of this recently established marine protected area.

  The integration of eDNA would complement ongoing conservation efforts by providing a non-invasive, standardised framework for long-term biodiversity assessment, while enhancing the scientific robustness of existing monitoring initiatives, like benthic habitat mapping and marine megafauna monitoring. It would support adaptive management of the park and improve understanding of species composition, including habitat connectivity and seasonal presence of data-poor species such as the African manatee, as well as other marine megafauna.

  In addition, eDNA analyses could generate valuable outputs including biodiversity mapping, identification of biodiversity hotspots, and seasonal and long-term comparisons across sites, as well as detection of shifts in community composition. This would also contribute to regional and global biodiversity monitoring initiatives by providing evidence of species presence, particularly for IUCN and CITES listed and nationally protected species.

  Given the intensifying pressures from artisanal and industrial fisheries, increasing port activity, illegal industrial pelagic fishing, and associated maritime traffic, alongside acoustic disturbance, coastal development, pollution, and mangrove deforestation, eDNA-based monitoring would provide a critical tool for tracking ecological change in a cost-effective and scalable way. Long-term data collection would bridge critical knowledge gaps and provide updated, reliable, and consistent documentation of species and habitats, enabling detection of rare, non-native, and invasive species, and supporting stronger enforcement and management strategies.

  Local fishers and community members already actively participate in ecological surveys, invasive species management, and ecotourism initiatives. This participatory framework would be extended to eDNA surveys, where they will be prioritised in sample collection and field support, helping to build local technical capacity and strengthen long-term scientific autonomy within the park.
